üìã Strategic Philosophy

Stack Early Wins: Begin with achievable campaigns that demonstrate competence, build membership, and establish credibility before tackling systemic issues.

Bipartisan Appeal: Frame issues to attract support across political lines where possible. Economic freedom, anti-corruption, and local control resonate broadly.

üí∞ Section 1: Economic Freedom
üí∞

Economic Freedom & Anti-Cronyism

Removing government-created barriers that protect established interests

7 Focus Areas
This pillar addresses government-created barriers that protect established interests from competition, raise costs for consumers, and limit economic opportunity for ordinary Americans. When big business uses government to crush small competitors, that's not free markets ‚Äî that's cronyism.
1.1 Medicare Competitive Bidding Program Reform
Moderate Viability

‚ö†Ô∏è The Problem

  • Drove small, local medical equipment suppliers out of business
  • Created regional monopolies for large national suppliers
  • Reduced patient choice and service quality
  • Bid prices so low suppliers can't maintain quality service
  • Exemplifies regulatory capture ‚Äî large players shaped rules to eliminate competition

üìã Policy Positions

  • Reform competitive bidding to ensure fair competition and preserve local suppliers
  • Quality Standards: Require meaningful quality metrics, not just lowest price
  • Geographic Access: Ensure rural and underserved areas have adequate supplier access
  • Small Business Set-Asides: Reserve portion of contracts for small and local suppliers
  • Transparency: Full disclosure of bid evaluation criteria and winner selection

üéØ Strategic Framing

For conservatives: This is crony capitalism ‚Äî big companies used government to crush small business competition.

For healthcare advocates: Patients suffer when local suppliers who know them are replaced by distant call centers.

For rural communities: This policy devastated small-town medical equipment providers.

Level
Federal (CMS regulatory reform or congressional action)
Opposition
Large DME suppliers benefiting from current system
Allies
Small business associations, rural health advocates, patient advocacy groups
1.2 Occupational Licensing Reform
High Viability

‚ö†Ô∏è The Problem

  • Over 25% of American workers now need government permission to work (up from 5% in 1950s)
  • Thousands of hours of training required for low-risk occupations
  • Barriers for people with criminal records trying to rebuild their lives
  • Military spouses prevented from transferring licenses when relocated
  • Wildly varying requirements by state (hair braiders: 2,100 hours in some states, zero in others)

üìã Policy Positions

  • Sunrise Review: Require evidence of public harm before creating new licensing requirements
  • Sunset Provisions: Existing licenses expire unless legislature reauthorizes with evidence of necessity
  • Reciprocity: Recognize licenses from other states, especially for military families
  • Criminal Record Reform: Limit ability to deny licenses based on unrelated criminal history
  • Least Restrictive Alternative: Use certification or registration instead of licensing where possible

Specific Targets

  • Hair braiders and cosmetology (excessive hours for low-risk work)
  • Interior designers (licensing exists in few states, no evidence of harm elsewhere)
  • Tour guides (speech-based profession, First Amendment concerns)
  • Florists (licensing in Louisiana struck down as unconstitutional)
  • General contractors (vary from zero to extensive requirements by state)
Level
State (each state controls its licensing)
Opposition
Licensed practitioners protecting their market
Allies
Institute for Justice, state think tanks, workforce development groups
1.3 Right to Repair
High Viability

‚ö†Ô∏è The Problem

  • Proprietary parts and diagnostic tools restricted to authorized dealers
  • Software locks that disable equipment after unauthorized repairs
  • Farmers unable to repair their own tractors (John Deere controversy)
  • Electronics designed with planned obsolescence
  • Hospital equipment that only manufacturer technicians can service

üìã Policy Positions

  • Parts Availability: Manufacturers must make parts available to owners and independent repair shops
  • Diagnostic Access: Diagnostic tools and software must be available at reasonable cost
  • Documentation: Repair manuals and schematics must be publicly available
  • Anti-Circumvention: Owners may bypass software locks for repair purposes without violating DMCA
  • Agricultural Priority: Farm equipment is critical infrastructure ‚Äî farmers must be able to repair their own equipment

üéØ Strategic Framing

For conservatives: This is property rights ‚Äî if you bought it, you own it, you can fix it.

For rural America: Farmers can't wait two weeks for a dealer technician during harvest.

For environmentalists: Repair extends product life and reduces waste.

For consumers: Reduces repair costs and increases competition.

Level
State (multiple states have passed or considering legislation)
Opposition
Manufacturer associations, Big Tech, agricultural equipment companies
Allies
Farm Bureau (in some states), consumer groups, repair shops, environmental groups
1.4 Civil Asset Forfeiture Reform
Moderate-High Viability

‚ö†Ô∏è The Problem

  • Property can be seized without criminal conviction ‚Äî or even charges
  • Burden of proof often falls on owner to prove innocence
  • Proceeds often go directly to the seizing agency, creating perverse incentives
  • Disproportionately affects low-income individuals who can't afford legal fights
  • Federal "equitable sharing" allows circumvention of state reforms

üìã Policy Positions

  • Conviction Requirement: Require criminal conviction before permanent forfeiture
  • Clear and Convincing Evidence: Raise burden of proof for any interim seizure
  • Redirect Proceeds: Forfeit proceeds go to general fund, not seizing agency
  • Reporting Requirements: Mandate detailed public reporting of all seizures
  • Federal Equitable Sharing Ban: Prohibit state/local agencies from circumventing state law through federal partnerships
  • Innocent Owner Defense: Strengthen protections for innocent owners
Level
State (for state forfeiture) + Federal (for equitable sharing)
Opposition
Law enforcement associations, prosecutors
Allies
ACLU, Institute for Justice, libertarian groups, criminal justice reformers
1.5 ESG & Corporate-Government Collusion
High Viability

‚ö†Ô∏è The Problem

  • ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) metrics used to pressure companies into political positions
  • State pension funds voting shares based on ESG ideology rather than fiduciary duty to retirees
  • Banks denying services to legal industries (energy, firearms) under ESG pressure
  • Government agencies using ESG as backdoor regulation without legislative approval
  • Asset managers coordinating to impose political agendas on companies
  • Credit agencies penalizing companies/states for political positions

üìã Fiduciary Duty Protection

  • Sole Financial Interest: State pension funds must vote shares solely for financial return, not ESG scores
  • Anti-Boycott Laws: Prohibit state contracts with companies boycotting legal industries (energy, firearms, agriculture)
  • Banking Access: Financial institutions receiving state deposits cannot discriminate against legal industries
  • Divestment Prohibition: Prohibit state funds from divesting based on political rather than financial criteria

üìã Transparency Requirements

  • ESG Voting Disclosure: Require disclosure of all ESG-based proxy votes by state-managed funds
  • Asset Manager Accountability: State funds must report on asset manager ESG activities
  • Ratings Transparency: ESG rating agencies must disclose methodologies and conflicts
  • Political Activity Disclosure: Require disclosure of coordinated ESG political activities

üéØ Strategic Framing

For conservatives: Stop woke capital. Protect legal industries from political discrimination.

For workers: Pension managers should maximize your retirement, not pursue political agendas.

For markets: Investment decisions should be based on financial merit, not political scoring.

For energy independence: Stop financial discrimination against domestic energy production.

‚úÖ Active Momentum

Texas, Florida, West Virginia, and 20+ other states have passed or are considering anti-ESG legislation. This is a proven winning issue with model legislation available and strong grassroots support.

Level
State (pension management) + Federal (securities regulation)
Opposition
BlackRock, major asset managers, ESG rating agencies, environmental groups
Allies
State treasurers, energy industry, agriculture, Second Amendment groups, free market advocates
1.6 Healthcare Price Transparency
High Viability

‚ö†Ô∏è The Problem

  • Hospitals charge wildly different prices for the same procedure ‚Äî sometimes 10x variation
  • Prices hidden until after treatment; no ability to comparison shop
  • Insurance companies negotiate secret discounts unavailable to uninsured
  • "Chargemaster" prices bear no relation to actual costs
  • Surprise bills from out-of-network providers at in-network facilities
  • Patients cannot make informed decisions without price information

üìã Upfront Pricing Requirements

  • Binding Price Quotes: Require binding price quotes before non-emergency procedures
  • Published Prices: All providers must publish prices for common procedures
  • Real-Time Estimates: Insurance portals must provide real-time cost estimates
  • All-In Pricing: Quotes must include all anticipated charges (facility, physician, anesthesia, etc.)

üìã Market Transparency

  • All-Payer Claims Databases: State databases of actual prices paid by all payers
  • Public Comparison Tools: Government or independent price comparison websites
  • Quality Metrics: Link price information to quality outcomes
  • Negotiated Rate Disclosure: Require disclosure of insurance-negotiated rates

üìã Consumer Protections

  • Balance Billing Ban: No surprise bills from out-of-network providers at in-network facilities
  • Price Cap for Emergencies: Cap emergency charges at reasonable multiple of Medicare rates
  • Direct Primary Care: Remove regulatory barriers to direct primary care practices
  • Cash Pay Options: Providers must offer cash-pay prices to uninsured patients

üéØ Strategic Framing

For conservatives: Free markets require price information. End healthcare's exemption from normal market forces.

For progressives: Protect patients from predatory pricing. Stop surprise medical bills.

For everyone: You can compare prices for everything else. Why not healthcare?

‚úÖ Existing Framework

Trump-era hospital price transparency rule partially implemented. No Surprises Act (2022) addresses some issues. Build on existing framework while expanding and strengthening enforcement.

Level
State + Federal (both have roles)
Opposition
Hospital associations, some insurers, providers benefiting from opacity
Allies
Employer groups, patient advocates, direct primary care movement, fiscal conservatives
1.7 Environmental Policy: Polluter Pays Without Crushing Innovation
Moderate-High Viability
üí° The Principled Framework

Pollution is a property rights violation ‚Äî your emissions affect others' air, water, and health. The polluter pays principle is correct. But compliance regimes often create massive fixed costs that favor incumbents and crush small innovators. Environmental regulation frequently becomes a tool of regulatory capture ‚Äî big corporations can afford compliance departments and actually want complex rules that keep out competitors. We need environmental protection that works without destroying economic opportunity.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Problem with Current Approach

  • Compliance costs are largely fixed ‚Äî same paperwork whether you're a garage inventor or Fortune 500
  • Small businesses spend disproportionate % of revenue on environmental compliance
  • Complex regulations favor companies that can afford lawyers and compliance officers
  • Process-based rules mandate specific technologies rather than outcomes
  • Permitting delays can kill small projects while large corporations wait them out
  • Big polluters often write the regulations (regulatory capture)
  • Focus on paperwork rather than actual environmental outcomes
  • Innovation penalized ‚Äî new approaches face regulatory uncertainty

üìã Core Principles

  • Polluter Pays: Those who create pollution should bear the cost of mitigation or remediation
  • Proportional to Impact: Regulatory burden should match actual environmental impact
  • Outcomes Over Process: Measure actual pollution, not how you operate
  • Technology Neutral: Set standards, let innovators find solutions
  • Small Business Protection: Compliance should not be a barrier to entry
  • Local Flexibility: Communities can set standards appropriate to their context
  • Transparency: Actual emissions data public; accountability through information

üìã Tiered Regulatory Framework

Scale requirements to actual impact ‚Äî the 80/20 rule (most pollution comes from few sources):

  • De Minimis Exemption: Below threshold emissions exempt from complex permitting (garage tinkerers, small workshops)
  • Simplified Tier: Small operations use streamlined permits, presumptive compliance, self-certification
  • Standard Tier: Medium operations follow standard permitting with reasonable timelines
  • Major Source Tier: Large industrial emitters face full regulatory requirements
  • Focus Enforcement: Concentrate resources on major polluters, not paperwork violations by small businesses

üìã Innovation Protection

  • R&D/Prototype Exemption: Experimental and development activities face lighter requirements than commercial production
  • Regulatory Sandbox: Temporary flexibility for testing new approaches with monitoring
  • Scale-Up Pathway: Clear process for growing from prototype to production
  • Outcome Equivalence: Novel approaches that achieve same environmental outcome as prescribed method are acceptable
  • Permit by Rule: Pre-approved categories for common small-scale activities

üìã Market-Based Mechanisms

  • Pollution Fees: Price emissions directly rather than mandate specific controls ‚Äî let market find efficient solutions
  • Revenue Neutral: Any pollution fees offset by reductions in other taxes (not new revenue source)
  • Tradeable Permits: Where caps needed, allow trading so reductions happen where cheapest
  • Environmental Bonds: Post bond for potential damage; market prices risk appropriately
  • Liability Over Regulation: Strong property rights and liability rules can substitute for some prescriptive regulation

üìã Climate-Specific Considerations

  • Acknowledge Reality: Human activity affects climate; this is not controversial science
  • Proportional Response: Small businesses contribute tiny fraction of emissions ‚Äî focus on major sources
  • Technology Solutions: Innovation will solve this faster than regulation ‚Äî don't strangle innovators
  • Energy Realism: Transition requires reliable alternatives, not just shutting down existing sources
  • Adaptation Investment: Prepare for changes that will occur regardless of mitigation
  • No Carbon Colonialism: Don't export dirty production to countries with no standards
  • Nuclear Included: Zero-carbon baseload power must be part of the solution

üìã Anti-Regulatory Capture

  • Incumbent Input Limits: Regulated industries should not write their own regulations
  • Small Business Voice: Ensure small business representation in rulemaking
  • Complexity Audit: Review regulations for unnecessary complexity that favors large players
  • Competitive Impact Analysis: Assess whether rules create barriers to entry
  • Revolving Door Limits: Restrict movement between regulators and regulated industries

üéØ Strategic Framing

For conservatives: Property rights ‚Äî pollution is a trespass. Market mechanisms over command-and-control bureaucracy.

For progressives: Actually protect the environment by focusing on real pollution, not paperwork. Stop letting big corporations write loopholes.

For small business: Compliance costs shouldn't be a barrier to entry. Regulations shouldn't be a moat protecting incumbents.

For innovators: Space to experiment and develop solutions. Technology will solve this faster than bureaucracy.

‚ö†Ô∏è Honest Tensions
  • Threshold gaming: Companies may structure to stay below thresholds ‚Äî need anti-evasion provisions
  • Cumulative effects: Many small sources can add up ‚Äî may need area-wide approaches
  • Monitoring costs: Outcome-based regulation requires measurement ‚Äî who pays?
  • Global competition: Strict domestic rules can push production to dirtier countries
  • Political economy: Carbon taxes are unpopular even when economically efficient
Level
Federal (air/climate) + State (water, land) + Local (nuisance)
Opposition
Large incumbents benefiting from regulatory moats, bureaucracies, some environmental groups
Allies
Small business, innovators, free-market environmentalists, property rights advocates
üåæ Section 2: Food & Health Sovereignty
üåæ

Food & Health Sovereignty

Freedom to make your own food and health choices

4 Focus Areas
Americans should have the freedom to make their own food and health choices, access products from local producers, and be protected from harmful substances that other developed nations have banned. Food freedom and medical freedom are fundamental rights.
2.1 Unpasteurized (Raw) Dairy Legalization
Moderate-High (State)

‚ö†Ô∏è The Problem

  • Federal law prohibits interstate sale of raw milk
  • Many states ban or heavily restrict intrastate sales
  • Small dairy farmers cannot sell directly to willing consumers
  • Raw dairy consumed for millennia; many countries allow regulated sale
  • Enforcement includes armed raids on small farms

üìã State-Level Positions

  • Legalize on-farm sales (consumer can inspect conditions)
  • Legalize retail sales with appropriate labeling and consumer acknowledgment
  • Allow herd-share arrangements (consumer owns portion of animal)
  • Reasonable food safety testing without prohibitive costs

üìã Federal-Level Positions

  • Repeal federal ban on interstate raw milk sales
  • Allow states to determine their own raw dairy policies
  • End FDA enforcement actions against small producers selling to informed consumers
Current State Status

Legal for retail sale: ~13 states | On-farm sales only: ~17 states | Herd-shares only: ~10 states | Illegal: ~10 states

Level
State (primary); Federal (long-term)
Opposition
Industrial dairy, some public health officials
Allies
Farm-to-Consumer Legal Defense Fund, Weston A. Price Foundation, libertarian groups
2.2 Local Food & Cottage Food Reform
High Viability

‚ö†Ô∏è The Problem

  • Cottage food laws (home-based food businesses) vary widely and are often restrictive
  • Small farmers face same regulatory burden as large operations
  • Direct-to-consumer sales restricted by rules designed for wholesale distribution
  • On-farm processing facilities subject to industrial-scale requirements
  • Small meat processors nearly eliminated by USDA inspection requirements

üìã Cottage Food Expansion

  • Expand list of allowed cottage food products
  • Increase or eliminate sales caps
  • Allow online and delivery sales, not just farmers markets
  • Clear labeling requirements instead of outright bans

üìã Farm-to-Consumer

  • Exempt direct farm-to-consumer sales from regulations designed for wholesale
  • Allow on-farm poultry processing up to reasonable limits (1,000-20,000 birds/year)
  • Support custom-exempt meat processing for farm-direct sales
  • Producer-only farmers markets with streamlined requirements

üìã Infrastructure Support

  • Support for small-scale USDA-inspected processing facilities
  • Mobile slaughter units for rural areas
  • Shared-use commercial kitchens for cottage food producers
Model Legislation: Food Freedom Acts

Wyoming, Maine, and Utah have passed Food Freedom Acts that allow direct producer-to-consumer sales with informed consent, exempt from licensing and inspection requirements, requiring only accurate labeling and producer identification while maintaining liability for adulterated or misbranded products.

Level
State (primary) + some federal action
Opposition
Industrial food processors, some health departments
Allies
Farm bureaus, local food movements, homesteading communities
2.3 Removing Toxic Chemicals from Food Supply
Moderate Viability

‚ö†Ô∏è The Problem ‚Äî US Permits What Others Ban

  • Red Dye No. 3 ‚Äî banned in cosmetics since 1990 but still in food; linked to cancer
  • Red Dye No. 40 ‚Äî linked to hyperactivity; requires warning labels in EU
  • Yellow No. 5 & 6 ‚Äî require warnings in EU; linked to behavioral issues
  • Brominated vegetable oil (BVO) ‚Äî banned in EU and Japan; neurological concerns
  • Potassium bromate ‚Äî banned in EU, UK, Canada, Brazil; classified as possible carcinogen
  • Azodicarbonamide ‚Äî banned in EU and Australia; used in US bread products
  • BHA/BHT preservatives ‚Äî restricted in many countries; potential carcinogens
  • rBGH/rBST (bovine growth hormone) ‚Äî banned in EU, Canada, Australia; linked to health concerns

üìã Immediate Bans (Clear Evidence of Harm)

  • Red Dye No. 3 (already banned in cosmetics ‚Äî extend to food)
  • Brominated vegetable oil
  • Potassium bromate

üìã Labeling Requirements

  • EU-style warning labels for artificial dyes linked to behavioral issues
  • Clear disclosure of all artificial additives
  • Country of origin labeling expansion

üìã Regulatory Reform

  • Review all GRAS (Generally Recognized as Safe) substances grandfathered from old regulations
  • End industry self-certification for new food additives
  • Adopt precautionary principle ‚Äî burden of proof on manufacturer
  • Harmonize with EU/international standards where appropriate

üéØ Strategic Framing

For parents: Protect children from additives linked to behavioral and health problems.

For consumers: Americans deserve the same protections as Europeans.

For conservatives: Informed consent ‚Äî let people know what's in their food.

Level
Federal (FDA regulatory action or congressional legislation)
Opposition
Food industry associations, chemical manufacturers
Allies
Consumer advocacy groups, parents' organizations, reformulating food companies
2.4 Medical Freedom & Informed Consent
Moderate-High (Varies by State)

üìã Core Principles

  • Individuals have the right to make their own medical decisions
  • Informed consent requires full disclosure of risks, benefits, and alternatives
  • Government mandates should be limited and subject to robust exemption rights
  • Medical decisions should remain between patient and provider
  • Access to treatments should not be contingent on accepting other treatments

üìã Policy Positions

  • Informed Consent: Require meaningful informed consent for all medical interventions
  • Exemption Rights: Protect religious, philosophical, and medical exemptions to mandates
  • Employment Protection: Prohibit termination based solely on medical treatment decisions
  • Educational Access: No medical requirements for public education beyond genuine public health necessities
  • Provider Rights: Protect healthcare providers who conscientiously object to procedures
  • Treatment Access: Right to try experimental treatments with informed consent
  • Medical Privacy: Strengthen protections; limit employer/government access to records
Level
Primarily state; some federal
Opposition
Public health establishment, some medical associations
Allies
Medical freedom organizations, religious groups, civil liberties advocates
üè† Section 3: Property Rights
üè†

Property Rights

Defending what you own from government and private overreach

2 Focus Areas
Property rights are foundational to liberty. Government and private entities increasingly infringe on what Americans can do with their own property. When you buy something, you should own it ‚Äî fully.
3.1 Homeowners Association Reform
High Viability

‚ö†Ô∏è The Problem

HOAs often exercise quasi-governmental power without corresponding accountability:

  • Mandatory membership with no opt-out option for existing communities
  • Broad, vague restrictions enforced arbitrarily
  • Fines and penalties with limited due process
  • Foreclosure authority for unpaid assessments (including minor amounts)
  • Selective enforcement based on board member relationships
  • Limited transparency in financial management
  • Elections subject to manipulation by management companies
  • Restrictions that conflict with state/federal law (solar panels, flags, etc.)

üìã Governance Reform

  • Require open meetings and access to financial records
  • Mandate competitive bidding for contracts
  • Prohibit board members from self-dealing
  • Require independent audits for HOAs above certain size
  • Term limits for board members

üìã Enforcement Limits

  • Cap fines; require escalating warnings before penalties
  • Prohibit foreclosure for fines (assessments only, with limits)
  • Require due process before any penalty (notice, hearing, appeal)
  • Statute of limitations on violations
  • Selective enforcement as affirmative defense

üìã Protected Rights

  • Solar panels and energy equipment (federal law exists but needs teeth)
  • Flag display (US flag already protected; extend to state/military)
  • Political signs (time-limited during elections)
  • Vegetable gardens (reasonable backyard use)
  • Disability accommodations (strengthen enforcement)
  • Religious displays (protect reasonable religious expression)

üìã Transparency

  • Mandatory disclosure to buyers before purchase
  • CC&R plain language requirements
  • Online access to all governing documents
  • Public reporting of enforcement actions

üéØ Strategic Framing

For conservatives: Property rights ‚Äî you bought it, you should be able to use it.

For homeowners: Protection from petty tyranny and arbitrary enforcement.

For everyone: HOAs exercise government-like power without government-like accountability.

Level
State (HOA law is state-specific)
Opposition
HOA management companies, some HOA boards
Allies
Property rights groups, homeowner advocates, real estate consumer groups
3.2 Eminent Domain Reform
Moderate-High Viability

‚ö†Ô∏è The Problem

  • Since Kelo v. City of New London (2005), "public use" stretched to allow takings for private development
  • Property can be taken and given to private developers if it might increase tax revenue
  • Low-income neighborhoods targeted for "redevelopment"
  • Compensation often inadequate (fair market value may not cover relocation costs)
  • Threat of eminent domain used to coerce below-market sales

üìã Policy Positions

  • Public Use Restriction: Limit eminent domain to true public use (roads, utilities, public buildings)
  • No Private-to-Private Transfers: Prohibit taking property to give to another private party
  • Just Compensation Plus: Compensation must include relocation costs, not just market value
  • Blight Definition: Tighten definition of "blight" to prevent abuse
  • Right of First Refusal: If taken property not used for stated purpose, original owner can repurchase
Level
State (many reformed after Kelo; some need strengthening)
Opposition
Developers, municipal governments, economic development agencies
Allies
Property rights groups, Castle Coalition, affected communities
3.3 Mineral Rights Protection
Moderate-High Viability

‚ö†Ô∏è The Problem

  • Mineral rights severed from surface rights on vast majority of American land
  • Most property buyers have no opportunity to purchase mineral rights ‚Äî already owned by corporations
  • Corporations sit on mineral rights indefinitely without productive use ‚Äî "mineral right squatting"
  • Similar market failure to patent trolls and domain name squatters
  • Landowners have limited rights when mineral owners access their property
  • Foreign corporations increasingly own American mineral rights
  • Concentrated ownership enables market manipulation and speculation

üìã Use-It-Or-Lose-It Provisions

  • Active Development Requirement: Mineral rights must show active exploration, development, or production within defined period (e.g., 10 years)
  • Holding Fees: Escalating annual fees for undeveloped mineral rights ‚Äî discourages pure speculation
  • Reversion to Surface Owner: Abandoned or long-inactive mineral rights revert to surface property owner
  • Bona Fide Development Standard: Token drilling/exploration doesn't satisfy requirement ‚Äî must be genuine commercial activity
  • Exemption for Active Leases: Rights under active lease with producing operator are protected

üìã Surface Owner Protections

  • Access Compensation: Fair compensation for surface disruption, not just mineral value
  • Restoration Bonds: Mineral operators must post bonds for full site restoration
  • Notice Requirements: Advance notice and negotiation before surface access
  • Damage Liability: Strict liability for surface damage, groundwater contamination
  • Reasonable Access Standards: Define "reasonable" to protect surface owner's use and enjoyment

üìã Market Access Reforms

  • Right of First Refusal: Surface owners have first right to purchase mineral rights when sold
  • Reunification Incentives: Tax incentives for reunifying mineral and surface rights
  • Disclosure Requirements: Clear disclosure of severed mineral rights in all property transactions
  • Anti-Concentration Limits: Prevent single entities from controlling dominant share of regional mineral rights
  • Foreign Ownership Restrictions: Limit or prohibit foreign ownership of mineral rights (national security)

üìã Anti-Speculation Measures

  • Transparency Registry: Public registry of all mineral rights ownership
  • Shell Company Disclosure: Require disclosure of beneficial owners behind corporate mineral owners
  • Transfer Taxes: Higher transfer taxes on speculative mineral right flipping
  • Small Landowner Protections: Enhanced protections for family farmers and small landowners

üéØ Strategic Framing

For conservatives: Property rights ‚Äî if you own the land, you should have opportunity to own what's under it. End corporate squatting.

For progressives: Environmental protection through landowner empowerment. End extractive industry exploitation.

For rural communities: Give families control over their land. Stop absentee corporations from dictating land use.

For energy independence: Active development requirements increase domestic production; speculation decreases it.

‚ö†Ô∏è Property Rights Balance

This policy must carefully balance protecting current property rights (mineral owners have legitimate ownership) with preventing market failures that harm both landowners and the broader economy. Focus on use requirements and access reforms rather than confiscation. Model on successful "use it or lose it" precedents in water rights, spectrum allocation, and mining claims.

Level
State (property law is state-specific)
Opposition
Oil/gas companies, mineral speculators, some property rights absolutists
Allies
Farm bureaus, rural landowners, environmental groups, energy independence advocates
üèõÔ∏è Section 4: Government Accountability
üèõÔ∏è

Government Accountability & Reform

Restoring accountability and limiting conflicts of interest

5 Focus Areas
Americans have lost trust in government institutions. Restoring accountability requires structural reforms that limit conflicts of interest, increase transparency, and return power to citizens. Those who govern should be accountable to those they govern.
4.1 Congressional Stock Trading Ban
High Viability

‚ö†Ô∏è The Problem

  • Members of Congress trade stocks while having access to non-public information
  • Exposed trades during COVID-19 pandemic (insider information on economic impact)
  • Committee members trading stocks of companies they regulate
  • Defense committee members investing in defense contractors
  • STOCK Act (2012) has proven inadequate ‚Äî weak enforcement, minor penalties

üìã Policy Positions

  • Full Trading Ban: Prohibit members and senior staff from trading individual stocks
  • Blind Trusts or Index Funds Only: Investments limited to diversified holdings without individual stock selection

4.5 Legislative Reform: Transparency, Sunset, and Accountability
Moderate-High Viability

‚ö†Ô∏è The Problem

  • Laws remain on books indefinitely ‚Äî never reviewed for effectiveness
  • Complex issues used to justify omnibus bills that escape scrutiny

  • Lobbyists insert provisions that would fail as standalone bills

Single-Subject Requirements

üìã Transparency Standards

  • Single Subject Rule: Each bill addresses one subject (many state constitutions have this)
  • Title-Content Consistency: Bill contents must match the title/stated purpose
  • Minimum Review Period: No vote until bill text available for 72 hours minimum
  • Read the Bills Act: Require attestation that member (or senior staff) has read the bill
  • Separate Votes: Amendments voted separately from underlying bill
  • Plain Language Summaries: Non-lawyer readable summary required for every bill

Universal Sunset Provisions

üí° Core Principle

Government should continuously justify its restrictions on liberty. Laws that cannot survive periodic review should not exist. Only constitutional provisions should be permanent ‚Äî everything else must earn its continuation.

üìã Automatic Expiration Framework

  • Default Expiration: All non-constitutional laws expire automatically after a set period
  • Tiered Renewal Periods:
    • Routine/Administrative: 5-year renewal cycle
    • Major Policy: 10-year renewal cycle
    • Foundational/Structural: 20-year renewal cycle
  • Renewal Requirements: Affirmative vote required ‚Äî no automatic continuation
  • Effectiveness Review: Before renewal, require assessment: Did the law achieve its purpose? At what cost?
  • Modification Opportunity: Renewal process allows amendments based on experience
  • Constitutional Exception: Only provisions enshrined in Constitution are permanent

üìã Sunset Review Process

  • Automatic Calendar: Laws placed on renewal calendar based on passage date
  • Review Committee: Dedicated committee reviews laws approaching expiration
  • Public Comment Period: Citizens can submit testimony on law effectiveness
  • Cost-Benefit Update: Updated analysis of costs vs. benefits since passage
  • Consolidation Option: Related expiring laws can be consolidated or simplified
  • Grace Period: 6-month grace period after expiration to allow renewal if delayed

Complex Issue Framework

üí° Addressing Legitimate Complexity

Some issues genuinely require multi-faceted solutions. The answer isn't to abandon single-subject rules ‚Äî it's to handle complexity transparently through staged implementation and component accountability.

üìã Complex Issue Handling

  • Package Designation: Legislature may designate a set of related bills as a "reform package"
  • Component Bills: Complex reforms broken into separate, single-subject bills
  • Sequenced Voting: Package components voted individually but in defined sequence
  • Conditional Enactment: Components may be conditioned on passage of related components
  • Independent Sunset: Each component has its own renewal cycle ‚Äî bad parts can expire while good parts continue

üìã Staged Implementation

  • Phase Gates: Complex reforms implemented in phases with review gates
  • Trigger Provisions: Later phases triggered by measurable outcomes from earlier phases
  • Abort Mechanism: Built-in authority to halt implementation if early phases fail
  • Component Accountability: Each phase/component has identified sponsor accountable for results
  • Public Dashboard: Transparent tracking of implementation progress and outcomes

For everyone: No more thousand-page bills no one reads. Complexity handled transparently, not hidden.

6.2 Individual Liberty & Freedom of Conscience
Moderate-High Viability
üí° Core Principle: Live and Let Live

Every individual has the right to live according to their own beliefs, identity, and choices ‚Äî and every individual has the right NOT to be compelled to affirm, participate in, or express beliefs they do not hold. Your freedom to be yourself does not create an obligation for others to validate you. This is the consistent application of liberty to all sides.

üìã Protected Individual Rights (Negative Liberty)

These are "leave me alone" rights ‚Äî government and others cannot interfere:

  • Marriage: Right to marry any consenting adult; government recognition of all marriages
  • Identity: Right to identify, present, and live as you choose
  • Belief: Right to hold any religious, philosophical, or personal beliefs
  • Expression: Right to express your identity and beliefs
  • Association: Right to associate with others who share your views
  • Privacy: Right to make personal decisions without government interference
  • Non-Discrimination by Government: Government services available equally to all

üìã Freedom of Conscience (Protected Refusal)

No one can be compelled to affirm beliefs they do not hold:

  • No Compelled Speech: Cannot be forced to use specific words, pronouns, or affirmations
  • No Compelled Participation: Cannot be forced to participate in ceremonies or events that violate conscience
  • No Compelled Belief: Cannot be punished for privately holding different views
  • Religious Exercise: Religious individuals and organizations can operate according to their beliefs
  • Creative Expression: Artists, writers, and creators cannot be compelled to create content that violates their beliefs
  • Professional Conscience: Professionals can decline services that violate deeply held beliefs (with referral obligation)

üìã Policy Applications

  • Marriage Equality + Religious Liberty: Government recognizes all marriages; religious institutions choose which to solemnize
  • Gender Identity: Live as you choose; others choose whether and how to acknowledge
  • Public Accommodations: General services available to all; custom expressive services protected
  • Employment: Cannot fire for identity; cannot compel affirmation training that violates beliefs
  • Education: Teach facts and multiple perspectives; do not compel children to affirm contested beliefs
  • Healthcare: Access to care for all; conscience protections for providers on specific procedures
  • Sports: Federalism ‚Äî let leagues, states, and institutions develop their own policies

üìã What This Is NOT

  • Not a license to harass: Leave people alone includes not targeting them for who they are
  • Not denial of existence: People exist and have rights regardless of others' beliefs about them
  • Not government-imposed orthodoxy: Government takes no position on contested metaphysical questions
  • Not majority rule on identity: Minorities don't need majority approval to exist peacefully
  • Not compelled approval: Peaceful coexistence doesn't require enthusiastic affirmation

üéØ Strategic Framing

For social conservatives: Your religious beliefs and conscience are protected. No one can force you to affirm what you don't believe.

For progressives: LGBTQ+ individuals have full legal equality and protection from government discrimination.

For libertarians: Maximum liberty for everyone. Your rights end where compulsion of others begins.

For everyone: We don't have to agree on everything to live peacefully together. Tolerance means tolerating difference, not demanding uniformity.

Campaign Selection Criteria

üìã Before Taking On a Campaign, Ask:

  • Can we define specific, achievable policy goals?
  • Do we have (or can we build) the expertise to be credible?
  • Are there natural allies we can coalition with?
  • Can we identify clear opposition and counter their arguments?
  • Will victory build our membership and credibility?
  • Does it align with our core mission and values?
  • Do we have the resources to sustain the campaign?
